01 · The Thesis

Stop renting traffic. Build a library.

The 2026 strategy doc is right: PPC drives the highest-intent calls and SEO builds organic visibility for the consulting arm. What it can't fund is differentiation. Every regional hauler runs the same Google Ads playbook. National competitors like RoadRunner and Rubicon out-spend Midwest Waste 10× on search. The way through is not to outbid them — it's to build something they can't replicate cheaply: a recurring, proprietary content layer that compounds in search, social, and AI answer engines simultaneously.

That layer is Trash Talk — a biweekly podcast hosted by Tyler & Greg, recorded on an iPhone in the cab of a truck — converted into long-form, immersive landing pages that become the new website. Not blog posts. Not transcripts. Pages designed for dwell time, scroll depth, and the kind of structured semantic content that Google's AI Overviews and Perplexity quote verbatim.

02 · Competitive landscape

The category is consolidating to its extremes. The middle is empty.

North American waste is splitting into two camps. Legacy giants — Waste Management (24% North American share, $22.1B revenue) and Republic Services (25% public share) — own the landfills, fleets, and routes. Asset-light tech disruptors — RoadRunner Recycling ($199M raised, AI-powered Waste Metering™ dashboards) and Rubicon Global (RUBICONSmartCity SaaS, vendor-agnostic procurement) — own the customer dashboards and the ESG narrative. Keter Environmental, PE-backed national managed services, sits in the middle. All five spend tens of millions a year acquiring customers.

None of them currently owns the position Midwest Waste is uniquely suited for: thirty years of operational truth, told in a credible human voice, on a digital surface that ranks and gets cited by AI. That's the gap. Trash Talk and the immersive landing pages are the instrument that takes it.

Podcast naming

Should it actually be "Trash Talk"?

A pun-perfect name walking into a very crowded room. Here's the reality check — and the alternative that earns the click without losing the wit.

The reality check

"Trash Talk" is the natural instinct — it's a perfect industry pun. The problem is discoverability. A search for "Trash Talk Podcast" currently returns:

  • A massive gaming podcast.
  • A 90-Day Fiancé recap show.
  • A sports betting show.

Trademarks are usually category-specific, so legal coexistence is plausible. But on Spotify and Apple Podcasts, Midwest Waste lands somewhere on page 10 — and the name itself becomes a discoverability tax that paid promotion has to claw back every week.

Four directions considered

The job is to bridge "gritty industrial work" and "high-level consulting" without sounding like either one alone. The shortlist:

  • Witty but professional — Haul Assets · The Refuse Report · Talkin' Shop (and Scrap) · Picking Up the Tab
  • Modern & industrial — The Waste Blueprint · Dumpster Divining · Managed Motion · The Haul Back
  • Midwest connection — The Heartland Haul · The Great Lakes Garbage Guide · Midwest Manpower & Managed Waste
  • High-energy / culture — We Love Garbage (great if the show leans behind-the-scenes; off-tone if the audience is CFOs and property managers)

The recommendation · "The Bottom Line on Waste"

One name, three reasons:

  • Double entendre. Refers to the bottom of the bin and the financial bottom line. Lands the industry pun without forfeiting the consulting authority.
  • Authority. Tells a potential client exactly what they'll get — expert advice on what waste actually costs and how to spend less on it.
  • SEO. Includes the keyword "Waste," which is what the buyer is actually typing. Easier to find for the people we want to find.
Double entendre B2B-credible Searchable Logo-friendly

Before locking the name

  • URL + socials. Confirm bottomlineonwaste.com (or similar) and the Instagram / LinkedIn / YouTube handles are clear.
  • Elevator-pitch test. "Check out our podcast, The Bottom Line on Waste" — does it land in conversation?
  • Logo synergy. The name has to set neatly under the Midwest Waste Consultants mark without crowding it.
  • Audience call. Educational show for clients (how to save money) or industry show for haulers (how to run a better fleet)? The name leans toward the first; the show can do both, but the name signals the lead.

Architecture plan

The Junkyard Jam payment system.

Engineered so the architecture — not the payment provider — sets the ceiling, with safety and integrity baked in from day one and validated under synthetic load before launch.

Capacity tiers

Day one starts at the simplest configuration — Stripe Checkout on a managed database — sized for realistic regional hauling volume. The platform is shaped so the limit moves with the architecture, not the payment provider: dropping in a queue lifts the ceiling to 1,000+ orders per hour, exponential backoff to 10,000+ per hour, and a Stripe limit increase carries it to enterprise volume. Each tier is a known move, not a rebuild.

Stripe 100 ops/s cap

The honest bottleneck. Stripe rarely fails. The system fails when the server can't process the success webhook fast enough. The architecture is built around that fact — webhook handlers are asynchronous by default, never synchronous.

Architecture & safeguards

The design separates day-one safety primitives — which ship the moment the site is live — from throughput moves that drop in cleanly when volume warrants them. Both belong in the architecture from the start:

  • Stripe Checkout, never raw cards. Day one. Card data goes from the customer's browser to Stripe and never touches our servers — PCI scope is offloaded entirely.
  • Webhook signatures verified on every request. Day one. Stripe signs every webhook; anything without a valid signature is rejected before it reaches a handler.
  • Idempotency keys on every charge. Day one. Network retries can never double-bill a customer.
  • TLS in transit, encryption at rest. Day one. Standard hosting hardening — there is no excuse not to ship this from minute one.
  • Async processing through a job queue. Drops in at 1,000+ / hr. Webhook arrives, gets queued, returns 200 immediately. Workers handle email, invoice, and CRM updates in the background.
  • Dead-letter queue + alerting. Drops in with the queue. Anything that fails three times lands in a queue that can be inspected and reprocessed. Nothing is lost silently.
PCI offloaded Signed webhooks Idempotent TLS + at-rest encryption Stripe Radar fraud rules

Pre-launch validation

Before a single real customer transacts, the whole stack runs through synthetic load:

  • 5× projected peak. k6 or Artillery hammer the checkout endpoint, the webhook receiver, and the order-confirmation flow simultaneously at five times expected launch-day volume.
  • 30-minute soak at 2× peak. Surfaces memory leaks and queue starvation that don't show up in short bursts.
  • Failure injection. Webhook responses are deliberately dropped, a worker is killed, and the database is partitioned to confirm the queue and idempotency keys recover the order without manual intervention.
  • Stripe test-mode dry run. Same code path as production, no real money moves, every alert rule fires on cue.
  • Production monitoring from minute one. Webhook latency, queue depth, payment-success rate, dead-letter count. If any of those drift, the team knows before the customer does.

Why this format wins SEO and AEO in 2026

1 · Semantic depth that competitors can't fake
30-minute conversations between two genuine subject-matter experts produce thousands of words of original, on-topic content per episode. Across 24 episodes that's hundreds of thousands of words of unique material — the kind of corpus that ranks for hundreds of long-tail queries no one is bidding on. PPC competitors can't compete with content they don't have.
2 · Dwell time and engagement signals
Interactive landing pages — accordions, flip cards, embedded clips, scroll animations — produce 3–5× the time-on-page of a standard blog post. Google has been weighting dwell time and meaningful engagement increasingly heavily since 2024. Shareable formats also generate referral links, the gold standard ranking signal.
3 · AEO — Answer Engine Optimization for AI Overviews and Perplexity
AI search engines (Google AI Overviews, Perplexity, ChatGPT browse, Claude) preferentially cite pages with structured Q&A schema, FAQ markup, and clear topical authority. Each Trash Talk landing page ships with PodcastEpisode + FAQ + HowTo JSON-LD. Episodes about specific topics ("how to size a roll-off dumpster," "what does a national waste broker actually do," "how senior cleanouts work") become the canonical citation source — quoted by name in AI answers.
4 · Local intent reinforcement
Episodes recorded in Sugar Grove, Aurora, Wheaton, and West Chicago — and tagged with location schema — feed organic visibility for the existing local landing pages. Each episode becomes a fresh signal that Midwest Waste is the active, relevant local authority. This is the single biggest unlock against the "Big Three" national haulers, who can't credibly claim local presence in a Fox Valley neighborhood.
5 · Compounding link equity
Each episode page links to relevant service hubs, location pages, and prior episodes. Within a year the site has 24+ deeply-linked editorial pages all reinforcing one another. This is how authority sites are built — not by hiring a link agency, but by producing things people want to link to.

How to think about $1,500/month

The 2026 strategy doc benchmarked content marketing at $10,500–$16,000 per month, bundled with PPC and paid social. This plan separates concerns: $1,500/month buys the content engine — two episodes, the immersive landing pages, monthly email, social clips, and ongoing updates on both sites. Paid acquisition (PPC, SEO ads, paid social boosts) is intentionally not bundled; it can be layered on later as the consulting pipeline matures, funded by the AI Dividend (~$15,500/mo in projected operational savings the strategy doc projects). The content library compounds either way.
